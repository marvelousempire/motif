# Scenario Flows

## What A Scenario Flow Means

Scenario Flow is a Motif term.

A Scenario Flow is a real-life situation that lets a person see how Motif could fit into their life before they need a technical explanation.

It is not just a use case.

It is not just a feature list.

It is a scene someone can picture.

The goal is for the person to say:

"Oh, wait. I can see how I would use that."

## Why Scenario Flows Matter

Some products are hard to explain because they are flexible.

Motif is one of those products.

Motif can be an idea system, a product system, an inventory system, a project system, a feature system, a workflow system, or a knowledge graph.

A Scenario Flow helps people understand Motif without forcing them to learn all the technical parts first.

## Scenario Flow Rule

A good Scenario Flow should:

- Start with a real-life situation
- Use language a regular person can understand
- Show the person inside the problem
- Reveal that Motif can change the lens
- Let the person imagine their own version
- Avoid sounding like a sales pitch

## Example 1: The Shopping Platform That Becomes Inventory

A person is looking at a shopping platform.

They see products, categories, attributes, and a cart.

At first, it looks like a normal store.

Then they realize:

- Products could become truck parts
- Categories could become truck sections
- Attributes could become condition details
- Cart could become a repair list

Suddenly the shopping platform is not only a store.

It could be an inventory system for a work truck.

That is Motif.

The engine stays the same, but the lens changes.

## Example 2: The Desk Full Of App Ideas

A person is sitting at their desk with notes everywhere.

They have app ideas, project ideas, feature ideas, and random tasks.

They do not need another blank notes app.

They need a way to turn messy ideas into structure.

Motif helps them map:

Intent → Idea → Feature → Action → Application → Purpose → Validated Execution

Now each idea has a valid intent.

Each feature has a keyword combo.

Each action can trace back to why it exists.

## Example 3: The CSV Full Of Ideas

A person has a spreadsheet full of ideas.

They upload it into Motif.

Motif lets them map the columns into Motif fields.

The spreadsheet becomes a graph of nodes.

Each row can become an idea, feature, action, tool, or project.

Motif asks for the missing intent if a row does not have one.

Now the person does not just have data.

They have a living idea system.

## Example 4: The Tool That Becomes A Meaningful Object

A person says, "I need a wet vac."

Motif does not stop at the object.

Motif asks what the object is intended to do.

The person says they need it to dry a floor.

Motif can classify that as:

```text
#WetVacDryFloor
```

Now the object has meaning.

It is not just a wet vac.

It is a node connected to an intent, an action, a purpose, and a validated result.

## Example 5: The Feature That Becomes A Build Plan

A person says, "I want something that helps me finish reports."

Motif can classify it as:

```text
#FinishReport
#FinishWriteReport
#SaveTime
```

Then Motif helps shape it into:

- Intent: Finish reports with less friction
- Idea: AI-assisted report workflow
- Feature: Report Finisher
- Actions: collect notes, draft sections, check missing parts
- Application: report completion tool
- Purpose: reduce unfinished work
- Validated Execution: user completes a report without losing context
